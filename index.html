About the book

This comprehensive compendium is a product of fifty-five years of continuous research. It contains 10,000 carefully selected Yoruba proverbs, fables and legends, categorized under 32 headings with appropriate translations into the English Language. The collection, which includes 77 pictorial illustrations, designed to aid the reader’s understanding of the Yoruba culture, is an invaluable reservoir of the time-honored wisdom of our elders.

The author also devotes a section of the book to a brief account of the origin, cultural heritage and population distribution of the Yoruba people worldwide, together with alphabetical listing of 130 notable Nigerians of Yoruba origin, who have written their names in gold on the pages of the Nigerian history. These heroes and heroines deserve to have their achievements celebrated.

It is expected that this book will be handy and useful to researchers, public speakers, songwriters, song composers, lecturers, students and lovers of Yoruba language and literature.

About The Author

Chief Stephen Ayodele Fatiregun

Biography

Stephen Ayodele Fatiregun was born in Lagos on Saturday 29th July 1939. He is a member of the Pioneer set (1953/58) of Ansar-Ud-Deen College, Isolo, where he sat and passed the Yoruba Language subject with distinction (‘A’ class) in the West African School Certificate Examination (WASCE) held in December 1958. Fatiregun is a Graduate of Business Administration, Harvard University, where he successfully completed the Harvard International Senior Managers Program in 1981.

Some yoruba proverbs

Bí a kò bá lè rà ọ̀pá tuntun fún arúgbó, a kìí gba t'ọwọ́ rẹ̀ sọnù.

If we cannot buy a new walking stick for an old man, it will be unfair to snatch and throw away the one he is currently using.

Ìkòkò tí ó bá máa báni kalé̩, kì í ti àárò̩ kán léti. Ojú tí ó bá máa báni kalé̩ kì í ti àárò̩ s̩e ipin.

The tip of the clay pot that will last long does not get chunked off very quickly. The eyes that will last till one’s old age does not bring out eye seeds in the prime of one’s life.

Oore tí a bá fé̩ s̩e fún e̩ran ò̩bo̩, pàbo ni yóò já si. Bó pé, bó yá, o̩mo̩ e̩ranko a máa fani laso ya

Any act of kindness extended to a monkey will be repaid with ingratitude. Sooner or later, the animal will tear off the dress of its benefactor.

E̩s̩in ò lè je̩ ìyà kú kí ìrùu rè̩ máà tún s̩e é wò ní àwùjo̩

If the horse was subjected to severe sufferings in its lifetime, it is only fair that its tail should, after its demise, be the cynosure of all eyes at any public gathering.
